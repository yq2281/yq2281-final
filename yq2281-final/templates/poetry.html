"The Lady of the Lake."

In dreams, I draw you out of the water and push my
breath into yours — oh, I’m a lotus-hearted fool, I’m
a yellow-blooded bastard. February opened her mouth
like a famished godling and swallowed me whole, and
again I went down to the Lake
(to the Lake,
to the Lake)
where there was no memory, and there was no time. Eat
mud, eat dirt — the way I prayed, you’d think I was a white
man’s wife. In the Lake, I saw three faces, but one was not
my own. In the Lake, I saw a dead man sleeping. First
there was the darkness, then there was memory, then there
was the darkness again. I pressed my knees to the mud and
wept like a famished king. “I will split you like the River
has split you,” said the Lady of the Lake. “I will cut you
like the River has cut you.” My grief was that of a mirror
world, but my sorrow was only my sorrow. I waded
through the silt bottom. I pushed my teeth through my
knuckles. I bit like a scoundrel. I turned my face toward a
stone-faced heaven, toward the steady drum and pour
of a plum-rain sky.

   


The Lady of the Lake, illustrated by Lancelot Speed for The
Legends of King Arthur and His Knights (Sir James Knowles, 1912)
